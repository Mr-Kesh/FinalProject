What

What's Universal Design

Universal Design (UD) is about making spaces, tools, and experiences that anyone can use, no matter their age, abilities, or background. It focuses on accessibility from the beginning so that everyone can enjoy and benefit equally.

Core Principles of Universal Design

Core Principles

  • Equitable Use: Designs should be useful to people with diverse abilities.
  • Flexible Use: Designs should support a range of individual preferences and abilities.
  • Simple & Intuitive Use: Interfaces should be easy to understand, regardless of user experience.
  • Perceptible Information: Content should be presented clearly, in ways that suit all users.
  • Tolerance for Error: Systems should help prevent mistakes and allow users to recover easily.
  • Low Physical Effort: Designs should minimize fatigue and effort for all users.
  • Size & Space for Use: Designs should accommodate a wide range of physical abilities and devices.

Applications of Universal Design

Examples in Everyday Life

  • Accessible Websites: Screen reader compatibility ensures usability for visually impaired users.
  • Closed Captioning: Subtitles provide access to multimedia content for hearing impaired users.
  • Physical Spaces: Ramps and tactile paths help individuals with mobility challenges.

Universal Design in Technology

  • Responsive Design: Websites adapt to mobile devices, tablets, and desktops.
  • Accessible Apps: Voice commands and gesture controls assist users with limited mobility.

Norman's Design Principles

Universal design works best when we follow some key ideas. Don Norman, a famous designer, shared principles that make things easier to use:

  • Affordance: Buttons should look like you can click them.
  • Feedback: When you click a button, it should tell you it worked (like changing color).
  • Constraints: A form shouldn't let you submit without filling required fields.
  • Visibility: Important things like links or buttons should be easy to see.
Examples of Norman's Principles in Action
Principle Example
Affordance Buttons shaped like clickable items.
